About Me

I am a researcher working at the intersection of fluid mechanics, biology, and robotics. My doctoral research at Brown University focused on using shrimp as a model organism for the development of bio-inspired underwater robots to explore terrestrial oceans and icy moons in the solar system. I study living organisms to develop engineering solutions for underwater exploration and sensing.

As a Distinguished Postdoctoral Fellow at the University of Texas Institute for Geophysics, I am working on new sensors for ocean measurements using phytoplankton morphology as a passive navigation mechanism, leading to shape-based navigation. This technology promises new understanding of microscale transport phenomena governing ocean mixing, particle aggregation, and biogeochemical processes.

Beyond my core expertise, I have worked on diverse problems in biofluid dynamics, from analyzing swimming biomechanics to exploring lactation fluid mechanics and advancing autonomous underwater vehicle design. My work bridges fundamental physics with practical applications, always maintaining focus on biological relevance and engineering innovation.
