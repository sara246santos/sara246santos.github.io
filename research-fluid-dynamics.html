Biological Fluid Dynamics

Understanding fluid interactions in marine organisms

Overview

My research in biological fluid dynamics focuses on understanding how marine organisms interact with their fluid environment at low to intermediate Reynolds numbers. Using quantitative flow visualization techniques such as Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV), I characterize the hydrodynamic forces and flow structures generated by small marine invertebrates during feeding, locomotion, and other behaviors. This work bridges fundamental fluid mechanics with ecological relevance, revealing how organisms leverage fluid dynamics for survival.

Feeding Flows in Barnacles

Barnacles are sessile crustaceans that feed by extending their cirri (feathery appendages) into the water column to capture food particles. By visualizing the vorticity fields around actively feeding barnacles, we can understand how these organisms generate feeding currents and how flow structures influence particle capture efficiency.

Escape Maneuvers in Shrimp

Shrimp perform rapid tail-flip escape maneuvers to evade predators. These high-acceleration movements generate strong vortical structures in the wake. Understanding the hydrodynamics of these escape responses provides insights into predator-prey interactions and the biomechanics of rapid locomotion at intermediate Reynolds numbers.

Vorticity visualization of a shrimp performing an escape jump maneuver

Mussel Aggregations and Flow Interactions

Mussels often live in dense aggregations where individual feeding currents can interact. Using PIV, we measure velocity fields around isolated mussels and groups to understand how aggregation affects feeding efficiency and flow patterns. The comparison between single and multiple mussels reveals how collective hydrodynamic interactions influence nutrient uptake in dense populations.
